FarmLinks Golf Club

The Course

Hurdzan-Fry Environmental Golf Design · Sylacauga, Alabama

Gently sculpted from three generations of Pursell family farmland, FarmLinks is Alabama's #1 ranked golf course and a top-100 course in the United States. Dramatic elevation changes, pristine fairways, and immaculate greens — every hole tells a piece of the land's history, from marble quarry rail lines to Civil War encampments to Prohibition-era whiskey stills. This is a course that rewards local knowledge and punishes overconfidence. Each hole has a name and a story. Learn both.

01
"The Farm"
Par 5
Details ↓
David Pursell designed this sprawling opener to give golfers a generous start. Many years ago, this land grew everything from cotton to corn.
Wide open off the tee — take your full swing and find the fairway. Excellent hole to get off to a good start before the course tightens up. Par here is money in the bank.
02
"Sawmill"
Par 4
Details ↓
When the Eureka Railroad came through, it stopped here to pick up lumber. The land was rich in hardwoods and pines. A small spring near the green powered the old sawmill operations and also refilled steam engines heading to the Eureka Marble Quarry.
Look for the little spring to the right of the cart path near the green — it's a piece of history. Play the hole straight and don't get cute. The approach demands precision.
03
"Baby Rattler"
Par 4
Details ↓
In 2012, David Pursell, his twin sons, and son-in-law came upon a baby rattlesnake coiled on the Copperhead tee — rattle shaking, ready to strike. Martin Pursell's 5-iron took care of it.
Watch your step in the rough — this isn't just a fun story. The Alabama heat means reptiles are active in late June. Play to the fat part of the fairway and stay on the cart paths when you can.
04
"Lower 40"
Par 4
Details ↓
Every farm has its "lower 40" — that long, expansive field requiring a full day's work. This is the course's #1 handicap hole. Roll up your sleeves.
This is the hardest hole on the course. Don't try to be a hero — make smart decisions, accept a bogey if it comes, and move on. The hole is won or lost off the tee.
05
"Hang Time"
Par 3
Details ↓
A 172-vertical-foot drop from the tips to the green. Hit it and watch it hang in the air for what feels like forever before the long plunge down.
Club down significantly — the elevation drop makes this play much shorter than the yardage suggests. Aim center-green, don't chase pins, and enjoy the view on the way down. One of the most visually spectacular holes in Alabama.
06
"Bootlegger"
Par 5
Details ↓
During Prohibition and long after, the woods right of the fairway were full of whiskey stills. Rusted remains and old still parts are still scattered in the trees today.
Don't slice it right — not because of out-of-bounds, but because of what you'll find in there. Worth a look if you hit it in the woods, but don't spend too long. Play the par 5 straight, in three shots, and move on.
07
"Big Daddy"
Par 4
Details ↓
An old farmhouse once stood on the small bluff to the left of the green behind the tire swing — lonely, tucked against the woods, like something out of a ghost story.
The bluff left of the green is more intimidating than it looks. A controlled tee shot to the center of the fairway sets up a straightforward approach. Don't overcook it trying to get close — par is a great result.
08
"Back It Up"
Par 3
Details ↓
Inspired by a hole at Black Diamond Ranch in Florida. When the tee complex was originally positioned closer to the green, David Pursell looked at it, shook his head, and said, "Back it up." And so they did.
Grab enough club, but looks can be deceiving — don't take too much. The hole is long, downhill, and open. Most will miss the green but leave a reasonable chip. Take your par and keep moving.
09
"Desoto"
Par 4
Details ↓
In 1540, Hernando de Soto and his band of fortune-seeking Spaniards passed through here searching for El Dorado, following the Coosa River. It's believed they camped for a winter in these very woods before heading southwest. Breastwork remains from their encampment can still be seen in the trees.
A great hole to finish the front nine strong. Solid drive sets up a manageable approach. Don't lose focus thinking about the history — there's still nine holes to play.
10
"Christine"
Par 5
Details ↓
Named for Lake Christine and the grand matriarch of the Pursell family. You can almost imagine her — fists up, knuckles shaking — reminding you not to let your guard down.
The lake is right off the tee and it demands respect. Don't get overconfident after a good front nine — this hole has humbled many. Lay up if needed, and play the par 5 in three disciplined shots.
11
"Lonely Pine"
Par 4
Details ↓
A lone pine stands centered in the fairway, put there for a reason. It's not uncommon for it to deliver a nasty kick.
Aim just left of the lone pine on your tee shot — it's the ideal line and avoids the kick. The pine is a landmark, not an obstacle, if you play it correctly. Don't challenge it head-on.
12
"Eureka Railroad"
Par 4
Details ↓
The old Eureka railroad tracks once cut through the property hauling pure white Sylacauga marble. You can still barely make out the old rail bed — it cuts from the northeast side of the green directly through the right greenside bunker and out toward the office building to the south.
The right bunker cuts right through where the old rail bed ran — a beautiful piece of design using the land's history. Play to the left side of the fairway to take the bunker out of play on your approach.
13
"Go For It"
Par 4
Details ↓
Not much of a backstory here. This is the "Just Do It" hole. Commit and execute.
FarmLinks is giving you permission. Pick your target, trust your swing, and go. This is one of the more aggressive holes on the course — reward aggression with a birdie opportunity.
14
"Swamp"
Par 4
Details ↓
This is the course's low country — the most level hole on the property. Pennywinkle Creek runs through the tree line to the left, home to every creature the Alabama South has to offer. The designers strongly suggest: if you hook it left into the woods, leave it there.
This is not a joke — do not go looking for your ball in those woods. Lose a stroke, play a provisional, and stay safe. The fairway is generous; keep it in play and the hole is very manageable.
15
"Showcase"
Par 3
Details ↓
A partner hole featuring specialized state-of-the-art turf varieties. The left bunker face features Discovery Bermuda — a type of Bermuda grass that only needs mowing once a year.
Aim away from the left bunker — that Discovery Bermuda is more fascinating to look at than to play from. The green is receptive; take your par and appreciate the turf science on display.
16
"Dana's Pick"
Par 4
Details ↓
Architect Dana Fry's personal favorite on the course. One of the Hurdzan-Fry partners loved this hole enough to name it after himself.
The fairway opens up nicely off the tee. Resist the temptation to cut the corner — the right side falls away sharply and the fairway bunker is positioned perfectly to catch anything too aggressive. Play left-center and stay out of trouble.
17
"Big Gulp"
Par 3
Details ↓
Like a giant 32-oz. cup, this body of water sits there yawning and waiting to be fed. It's a fairly easy hole — you just need to get the thought of hitting it fat out of your mind.
The only thing standing between you and a good score is your own head. The yardage is what it is, the distance isn't deceiving, and the carry is manageable. Commit fully to your club selection and make a confident swing. Don't let the water talk you out of it.
18
"Orchards"
Par 5
Details ↓
Long before this was a golf course, this expansive closing hole was home to sprawling apple and fruit orchards. Hamilton Place — the original Pursell family farmhouse built before the Civil War — stands just across the road from the tee box, visible to your left throughout the hole.
End the round strong. Hamilton Place watching over your left shoulder adds some gravitas to the finishing hole. The par 5 is reachable in two — give yourself a birdie chance to close out the round. Whatever happens, you'll remember playing in the shadow of a pre-Civil War farmhouse.
